100 PRISONERS

A simulation of the famous mathematical probability riddle.

What is the 100 Prisoners Problem?

The 100 prisoners problem is a mathematical problem in probability theory and combinatorics.

The Setup: 100 prisoners are numbered 1 to 100. A room contains a cupboard with 100 drawers. The director randomly puts one prisoner's number in each closed drawer.

The Challenge: The prisoners enter the room, one after another. Each prisoner may open and look into 50 drawers. They must leave the room exactly as they found it. If every prisoner finds their own number, all prisoners are pardoned. If even one fails, all die.

The Strategy: Random guessing gives a near-impossible survival rate of (1/2)^100 ≈ 0.0000000000000000000000000000008. However, there is an optimal strategy:

  1. Prisoner i opens drawer i.
  2. If the drawer contains their own number i, they are done.
  3. Otherwise, the drawer contains number j. The prisoner next opens drawer j.
  4. They repeat this until they find their number or run out of attempts (50).

Because the random placement forms "cycles", this strategy ensures that if the longest cycle is 50 or less, all prisoners will survive. Surprisingly, this yields a survival probability of about 31.18%!
